Probability and Distributions

From uncertainty intuition to practical probabilistic modeling

Article 4 of 7 · Statistical Foundations

Alerts and signals are evidence, not verdicts. Probability helps quantify how much a signal should change your belief.

Core Probability Concepts

An event belongs to a sample space \(\Omega\). Probability measures long-run frequency under repeated conditions.

Conditional probability updates the universe of possible outcomes:

\[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} \]

Bayes' Theorem in Decision Systems

Bayes inverts conditional direction, moving from signal given cause to cause given signal:

\[ P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)\,P(A)}{P(B)} \]

Example: if delay base rate is 10%, alert sensitivity is 70%, and false-alert rate is 20%, then:

\[ P(Delay \mid Alert) = \frac{0.70 \times 0.10}{0.70 \times 0.10 + 0.20 \times 0.90} = 0.28 \]

An alert almost triples delay probability (10% → 28%), but still does not imply certainty.

Discrete Distributions

Bernoulli: binary outcome, success/failure.

Binomial: number of successes in \(n\) independent trials.

Poisson: number of events in a fixed interval, especially for low-frequency counts.

Continuous Distributions

Normal: central in many measurement and averaging contexts.

Exponential: waiting-time model with memoryless property.

Uniform: equal likelihood across an interval.

For continuous variables, \(P(X=x)=0\). Meaningful probability is computed over intervals.

Central Limit Theorem

Regardless of the original distribution, sample means approach normality as sample size grows:

\[ \bar{X} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right) \]

This is a core foundation for confidence intervals and inferential procedures.

Practical Takeaways

  • Use conditional probability to reason with evidence.
  • Always account for base rates before taking action.
  • Select distributions based on variable type and generation process.
  • Use CLT-backed reasoning for sample-mean based inference.
